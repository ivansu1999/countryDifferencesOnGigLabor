The United States: Shift of Policy

The US government has been jumping back and forth on policies related to 'gig' workers and contractors. Due to the growing popularity of companies such as Uber, Doordash, and Lyft in the United States over the past decade, legal battles have been heatedly fought over the classification standards of employees.

Policy of Each US Presidency

Obama administration:

  • Employee misclassification cases were actively enforced, yet there were no new regulations to interpret employee status.
  • In 2015, guidance was issued stating employment relationships should be measured more by workers' economic dependency on employers and less by the amount of control exerted over them. (D. Shepardson and N. Bose, Reuter)


Trump administration:

  • Withdrew the guidance in 2017, considering it to be too broad.
  • The National Labor Relations Board concluded in 2019 that Uber drivers should not be considered employees under federal labor law given their freedom in their workplace.
  • In September 2020, a DOL proposal made it easier to classify workers as independent contractors.
  • In January 2021, the administration finalized a rule under the Fair Labor Standards Act that reflected the proposal.


Two weeks after the finalization, Trump left the office, and four months later the administration of Joe Biden blocked the Trump rule.

Texas Response to Biden Administration

A U.S. district judge in Texas in March 2022 blocked the Biden administration withdrawal and ordered the Trump rule to take effect. The Justice Department appealed.

In June 2022, the DOL said it would develop a proposed rule on determining employee or independent contractor status. It held forums in June to hear from workers and companies. (D. Shepardson and N. Bose, Reuter)
