Global Gig Economy and Labor

An introduction to the global view of gig economy.

In today's day and age where we are increasingly more reliant on technology and the uber convenience of the mobile industry, the nature of workforce is changing alongside. The emergence of the platform economy has been exponentially growing over the recent years; especially as a byproduct of the global crises like the Covid-19 pandemic, the gig economy has become a staple in our daily life. The pandemic restructured our idea of work with the temporary shutdown of so many businesses; the following stay-at-home guidelines further interfered with most people's ability to take part in the traditional economy. While the workers have more freedom on where and when to work, the lack of structure also denotes the lack of worker's labor rights that are usually protected by a formal employee by law. This project tries to collect and analyze related policies to better understand the geographies of platform work, on whether or not a gig worker is an employee or an independent contractor, and the ramifications of those classifications. Our project will present a broad world map of global digital platform GDP per country, then two case studies of UK and US, visualizing key legislation events on gig workers to show the complexity of law, policy, and economy of gig workers, and definitions and populations of gig workers in key countries. We aim to provide an overview information for the public on platform economy and its possible impact, help gig workers to gain knowledge on labor standards, and encourage policy makers and scholars to pursue further discussion.

Gig Worker: A Long-going Debate

The debate on whether or not a gig worker is an independent contractor or a formal employee is increasing with the emergence of a gig worker economy. There is a lot of confusion on how these workers are classified.

The dangers of misclassification lies in the fact that when workers can be undergoing wage theft by not being covered by federal wage worker protections and regulations such as minimum wage, paid sick days, health insurance, anti-descriminatory precautions, retirement benefits, and more.

Policies in Different Countries

In EU Parliament, a new proposal is being discussed to classify gig workers as formal employees under these conditions, The platform should:

  • Determine the pay
  • Require workers to follow rules regarding appearance
  • conduct toward clients or performance of the work
  • Use electronic means to supervise and assess job performance
  • Restrict work times or the freedom to turn the app off
  • Require exclusivity or noncompetition

The UK came to a conclusion on classifying gig workers as “workers”, an intermediary sector between formal employee an formal self-contractor. This classifications ensures certain protections and rights such as minimum wage, paid leave, and whistle-blower protections, but not protecting other securities such as unemployment benefits (SHRM). Meanwhile, Singapore also accepts 12 recommendations made by the Advisory Committee on Platform Workers to protect its gig workers on a nation wide scale. This includes enhancing housing and retirement adequacy, better representation of workers, and ensuring financial compensation in the case of a work injury (Ministry of Manpower - Singapore Government).

The United Kingdom: Uber vs. Gig Workers

In the UK, a 2021 Supreme Court ruling has created the potential for an overhaul of the current seemingly exploitative system. The ruling hasn't resulted in any new legislation yet, but it forced Uber to acknowledge “that its drivers are workers, not independent contractors” (CNBC).

This hard-won ruling concluded a five-year legal battle in which Uber repeatedly appealed the 2016 U.K. employment tribunal decision that called for Uber to reclassify their drivers as workers. Eventually, Uber made a final appeal to the Supreme Court after losing each previous appeal in Britain's courts and lost once again, which is a major win for gig workers in the U.K. who are now hoping for a trickle-down effect across the larger gig economy.

The Future of Gig System

Uber is not alone in their desire to keep drivers as independent contractors. Most major platform economy companies have taken advantage of the minimal responsibility that employing independent contractors creates. The question of whether gig work, as a whole, is beneficial or exploitative still stands, but the unceasing efforts on the part of these major companies to keep the system as is remains concerning.

The flexible nature of platform work is undoubtedly a draw for most gig workers and that is the seemingly selfless argument Uber utilized throughout the legal battle, but there is no doubt that the final decision isn’t going to easily translate into a needed overhaul of the system. There is still the matter of cost to the companies that this decision has the potential to inspire.

"The platforms are going to fight back because it really cuts into their revenue."

The United States: Shift of Policy

The US government has been jumping back and forth on policies related to 'gig' workers and contractors. Due to the growing popularity of companies such as Uber, Doordash, and Lyft in the United States over the past decade, legal battles have been heatedly fought over the classification standards of employees.

Policy of Each US Presidency

Obama administration:

  • Employee misclassification cases were actively enforced, yet there were no new regulations to interpret employee status.
  • In 2015, guidance was issued stating employment relationships should be measured more by workers' economic dependency on employers and less by the amount of control exerted over them. (D. Shepardson and N. Bose, Reuter)


Trump administration:

  • Withdrew the guidance in 2017, considering it to be too broad.
  • The National Labor Relations Board concluded in 2019 that Uber drivers should not be considered employees under federal labor law given their freedom in their workplace.
  • In September 2020, a DOL proposal made it easier to classify workers as independent contractors.
  • In January 2021, the administration finalized a rule under the Fair Labor Standards Act that reflected the proposal.


Two weeks after the finalization, Trump left the office, and four months later the administration of Joe Biden blocked the Trump rule.

Texas Response to Biden Administration

A U.S. district judge in Texas in March 2022 blocked the Biden administration withdrawal and ordered the Trump rule to take effect. The Justice Department appealed.

In June 2022, the DOL said it would develop a proposed rule on determining employee or independent contractor status. It held forums in June to hear from workers and companies. (D. Shepardson and N. Bose, Reuter)
